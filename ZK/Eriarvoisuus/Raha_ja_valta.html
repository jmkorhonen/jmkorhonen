Money is a claim to wealth, but not wealth itself. Money merely transfers ownership of wealth, without changing its overall magnitude. Frederick Soddy, the 1921 Nobel Laureate in Chemistry, lucidly captures this distinction,
defining money as ‘a token certifying that the owner of it is a creditor of the general community and entitled to be repaid in wealth on demand’ (Soddy, 1926, p. 134), or an ‘acknowledgement of the community’s indebtedness’ (Soddy, 1926, p. 75).

As a claim to wealth, money is a negative magnitude, taking the opposite sign of material wealth: to possess money indicates that one is owed but does not own a sum of wealth to which one is entitled. Money is both a unit of account expressing abstract value (i.e. quantified credit and debt relations) and a means of storing and transporting this abstract value (Ingham, 2004a, p. 70). Money, like capital, is an abstract social relation that can be embodied and represented in concrete things. Whereas money constitutes a power over the lives and labor of other people, wealth can be regarded as a product of power over nature.

Ingham, G.K. (2004a) The Nature of Money (Malden: Polity Press). V:2004:MoneyNatureOf-Ingham

Soddy, F. (1926) Wealth, Virtual Wealth and Debt: The Solution of the Economic Paradox (London: George Allen & Unwin). V:1926:Wealth-Soddy
