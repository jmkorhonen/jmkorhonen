Weapons expert Gary Milhollin says that "a single mistake in design could wreck the whole project."

Mueller 2009:loc3113: Los Alamos research Director Younger: It is simply "wrong to assume that nuclear weapons are now easy to make," he says, expressing his amazement at "self-declared 'nuclear weapons experts,' many of whom have never seen a real nuclear weapon," who "hold forth on how easy it is to make a functioning nuclear explosive." Information is readily available for getting the general idea behind a rudimentary nuclear explosive, but none of this is detailed enough to "enable the confident assembly of a real nuclear explosive."

Younger disagrees with the statement that crude gun-type weapons would be easy to make:

"Another challenge ... is how to choose the right tolerances. 'Just put a slug of uranium into a gun barrel and shoot it into another slug of uranium' is one description of how easy it is to make a nuclear explosive. However, if the gap between the barrel and the slug is too tight, then the slug may stick as it is accelerated down the barrel. If the gap is too big, then other, more complex, issues may arise.

All of these problems can be solved by experimentation, but this experimentation requires a level of technical resources that, until recently, few countries had. "

Stressing the "daunting problems associated with material purity, machining, and a host of other issues," Younger concludes, "to think that a terrorist group, working in isolation with an unreliable supply of electricity and little access to tools and supplies" could fabricate a bomb or IND "is far-fetched at best." (Younger 2007, 2009:144, 146)

In addition, the bomb makers would not be able to test the product to be sure they were on the right track.
- Hiroshima bomb was not tested, but during the Manhattan project, scientists and engineers spent years testing not only the gun device itself, but the trigger for the chain reaction, the casting and machining of the uranium metal in order to detect impurities in the product and to avoid fires and criticality accidents during production, and different configurations of material to determine how it would behave - a project that led to the death of one of the physicists. (Linzer 2004a; Mark et al. 1987:64; G. Allison 2007; Levi 2007b)
