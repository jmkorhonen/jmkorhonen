Very good Table 1: Mechanisms for persistence of technology from economic history literature:
- Complementaries between existing technology and new technology (lowers the cost of adopting the new technology; the more techs in initial tehh set, the more new techs there will be that are complementary)
- Recombinations of old technologies to make new technology (new techs novel combinations of old techs; the greater the # of old techs, the greater the # of possible combinations)
- Feedback from technology to science (when techs "work", this gives new evidence to scientists to test theories why they work. Science will in turn be used to make further innovations in technique)
- Feedback from technology to lower access costs for knowledge (the more advanced are certain "access" techs, the easier it is to obtain general scientific knowledge, which then lowers the cost of innovation and adoption; examples include alphabetization, Arabic numerals, Gutenberg printing etc)
- Spillover of tech from one sector to another
- Economies of scale
- Economies of scope of General Purpose Technologies
- Feedback from technology to improved lab equipment
- Learning by doing

Search for new designs and constraints in evolutionary theory (Metcalfe 1998)

The design space of possible technologies is so vast that we cannot hope to explain development in terms of random search. It is inefficient, it is too slow, it is not cumulative, it would be productive of an endless sequence of Heath-Robinson devices (Dennett, 1995). To make progress we need to put bounds on progress and consider only limited regions of design space, so the search for new designs normally follows established paths only occasionally jumping to new channels. ...
...the role of non-economic and non-social constraints in the development of new improved designs: constraints over and above those which bind because designs must be both useful and profitable if they are to be capable of being adaptations. These are the constraints of design which arise out of the inner logic of the design process in relation to the materials used, the production methods employed and the very configuration of the component parts of the artefacts in question.
- there is also a parallel between design constraints and paradigms in the Kuhnian sense (see e.g. Dosi 1982, DeLiso and Metcalfe, 1996)

For design constraints in modern evolutionary biology, see Gould and Lewontin, 1979; Maynard Smith et al. 1985): from positive viewpoint constraints help design, as they help indicate the possibly narrow paths forward. From a negative viewpoint thye limit what is possible, create interdependencies which are necessarily costly to overcome, and they help explain inertia and resistance to further innovation in the development of technologies and organizations

Gould, S. and Lewontin, R., 1979, ‘The Spandrels of San Marco and the Pan Glossian Paradigm: A Critique of the Adoptationist Programme’, Proceedings of the Royal Society, Vol. B 205, pp. 581-98.

Maynard Smith et al, 1985, ‘Developmental Constraints and Evolution’, Quarterly Review of Biology, Vol. 60, pp. 265-287.

The historian George Basalla has argued that technology is not the servant of science and that necessity is not the mother of technological invention

Connor (2004) argues that the invention of anesthesia in the 1800s was not a response to necessity; for many, anesthesia was not a solution to a pressing need, and Victorian doctors were not engaged in a relentless search for ways to reduce the pain of surgery. Prior experiments in nitrous oxide and ether had been made and both substances were available before the clinical introduction of anesthesia (ether in Boston in 1846 and choloroform in Britain in 1847). Several decades after the announcement of anesthesia, some Victorian surgeons still considered it an unnecessary luxury.

Singh and Fleming (2009): creativity as an evolutionary search process across a combinatorial space; other researchers of that view include Campbell (1960), Romer (1993), Weitzman (1998), Simonton (1999)
- in the variation phase, people generate new ideas through combinatiorial thought trials
- the idea that novelty is a new combination is at least as old as Adam Smith (1766)
- novel technologies can almost always be traced to combinations of prior technologies (Basalla 1988)
- Science, music, language, art, design, manufacturing, and many other forms of creative endeavor have been described similarly (Gilfillan 1935, Romer 1993, Weitzman 1998)
