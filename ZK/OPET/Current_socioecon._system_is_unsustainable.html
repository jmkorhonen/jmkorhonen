The roots of our predicament


  • Our socioeconomic system is a victim of its own success
  • For the last 200 years, human life has, on average, changed markedly for the better
  • However, success breeds complacency
  • We are particularly bad at recognizing limits
  • The most pressing limits now are climate crisis and ecosystem destruction
  • We may be able to lick the first with a World War-level effort - but what of the longer term?

Ideological underpinnings


  • Our current world-system is CAPITALISM, meaning private ownership and unlimited accumulation of capital (productive) goods
  • We have very few limits on accumulation (although more than we used to have: e.g. slavery is illegal)
  • The ideological foundations of today's problems have been laid over the last 200 years:
    • Superficial readings of e.g John Locke and Adam Smith are even today used to buttress claims of assumed supremacy of property rights and "free markets"
      • In reality, both philosophers argued for considerably more nuanced policies
      • e.g. for Smith, free markets were a solution to structural inequalities of late 1700s society; but the world changed and today "free markets" do more to perpetuate structural inequality
      • V:2017:AndersonPrivateGovernment


1900s - growth is invented


  • Economics implicitly assumed limits:
    • Declining marginal productivity of LAND means production could not increase in perpetuity
  • However, in the early 1900s limits were abstracted away from the economics profession
    • Post-Second World War economic boom; "golden age" for the U.S.
    • GDP became widespread measure, after its success in the war
    • By the 1960s, Western governments began to target economic growth as a key policy measure, replacing e.g. full employment
    • New theories of economic GROWTH: Solow (1956) assumed LAND away, because "this felt like a natural assumption to make" (p. 67) V:1956:EconGrowth-Solow
    • Assumed that capital could substitute for land
    • Extremely influential theory; did not contain any limits

The 1973 hiccup


  • Increasing awareness of environmental consequences of growth, coupled with the 1973 oil crisis, brought the first major wave of doubt: can growth continue forever?
  • 1972 Limits to Growth: extremely controversial, even today - even though its key conclusion, indefinite growth is unlikely to be feasible, is easy to see from a simple exponential equation
  • Provoked a major backlash
  • During the 1980s, numerous economists occupied themselves on theories that could permit indefinite growth
  • Paul Romer presented what became the most influential; noted later that a desire to find a theory that would allow indefinite growth was a major motivation V:2019:JonesOnRomer

"Of course growth can go on"


  • Today, most mainstream economists refer to Romer when saying that indefinite economic growth is totally possible
    • note what happens here: a theory that permits indefinite growth becomes a proof that indefinite growth happens
  • But economics has been detached from physical reality...


Crash course in thermodynamics


Laws of thermodynamics, paraphrased

First Law: You can't get anything without working for it.
Second Law: The most you can accomplish by work is to break even.
Third Law: You can't break even.

The second law essentially states that you can make bacon from a pig but cannot make a pig from bacon.

In practice


Everything we need to do requires energy and materials, and involves conversion of high quality inputs into lower quality outputs.

What does this mean:

  • Traditional economics tacitly assumes processes are reversible and that we live in a limitless world
  • But there are very fundamental limits

Thermodynamics rules


“The law that entropy always increases holds, I think, the supreme position among the laws of Nature. If someone points out to you that your pet theory of the universe is in disagreement with Maxwell's equations - then so much the worse for Maxwell's equations. If it is found to be contradicted by observation - well, these experimentalists do bungle things sometimes. But if your theory is found to be against the Second Law of Thermodynamics I can give you no hope; there is nothing for it but to collapse in deepest humiliation.”

Arthur Stanley Eddington, New Pathways in Science

What does this mean:

  • If economic theory does not account for thermodynamics, especially the second law, we should be very careful before using it on a non-infinite planet
  • CAUTION: THEORY ASSUMES REVERSIBILITY OF PHYSICAL PROCESSES. NOT FOR USE IN FINITE WORLDS.

We are locked in a capitalist treadmill


  1. Central objective: increasing accumulation of wealth (capital) to a relatively small group of people at the top of the social pyramid.
  2. Tendency to drive those who have to work for a living to wage labour, into jobs that will be lost unless quantity or quality of production constantly increases.
  3. Intercorporate rivalry demands corporations to use accumulated wealth for new, revolutionary technologies that either expand production or radically increase its quality.
  4. Needs are manufactured so that humans' desire for more is never satisfied.
  5. Governments become increasingly responsible for maintaining economic growth, e.g. to ward off societal unrest.
  6. Media and education institutions become part of the treadmill, helping to reinforce its priorities and values.

After Bellamy Foster, J. (1995), Global Ecology and the Common Good. Monthly Review 46(9) V:1995:FosterTreadmill
