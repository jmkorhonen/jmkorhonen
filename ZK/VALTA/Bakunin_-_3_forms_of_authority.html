Bakunin - 3 forms of authority

Created Thursday 27 August 2020
@2020 @ZK



Bakunin, in What is authority?

"In matters of boots I will defer to the cobbler"

Bakunin describes three forms of authority:

  1. The authority of nature, such as the law of gravity or rise and set of the sun
  2. The authority of expertise, such as a shoemaker or architect within their field
  3. The authority of social power, such as the state, or boss, or other rulers.

Bakunin holds that against the first, struggle is inherently pointless; any apparent attempt only reinforces the correctness of those laws. The second he views as valuable butthat it should be regarded with proper scepticism. You listen to experts to the degree your trust in them is warranted, but you aren't required to follow them.

The third is of importance to anarchists. This is where hierarchies enter the picture: people are given the power to rule over others. And it's important that authorities of expertise don't turn into authorities of power: we want anarchy, not meritocracy.


Hierarchy doesn't mean having more or less of an abstract quality, and it is important to distinguish something here: the MD is not more correct because they are an MD: they are more correct because what they say is more true. It would be the same if the PhD was correct -- the truth doesn't come from the person saying it, as good ideas can come from anyone and an idea or piece of knowledge is completely detatched from who has it.

Tldr: something about medicine isn't true because an MD said it instead of a PhD, it's true because it's true. Regardless, this isn't really the kind of thing I mean in the question, I meant more hierarchies of power, which is really what anarchists are against.
