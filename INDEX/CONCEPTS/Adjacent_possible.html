Adjacent possible

#Notes - technological possible

Keywords:
*adjacent possible*
*complexity*
*constraints*

Constraint creation moderating adjacent possible, somehow... There seems to be little thought about how the adjacent possible is constrained, even though adjacent possible as a term has been used to some extent.

Adjacent possible is implicit in recombinative view of innovation - it might be defined as the space of all the recombinatory possibilities?

Related to concept of technological frontier known as "adjacent possible" - i.e. Stuart Kauffman's concept that has been postulated to technological arena by e.g. Steven Johnson (Where good ideas come from, 2010). Johnson described it once as

"The adjacent possible is a kind of shadow future, hovering on the edges of the present state of things, a map of all the ways in which the present can reinvent itself."

According to Johnson, the adjacent possible "captures both the limits and the creative potential of change and innovation."

In a Wall Street Journal essay (Johnson, S. (2010) The Genius of the Tinkerer. Wall Street Journal, Sept. 25, 2010) and also on his book, Johnson defines adjacent possible according to Kauffman: for example,in the case of prebiotic chemistry, adjacent possible defined all the molecular reactions that were directly available in the prmordial soup.

He writes,

"The strange and beautiful truth about the adjacent possible is that its boundaries grow as you explore them. Each new combination opens up the possibility of other combinations."

Kauffman has once stated (interview with The Edge, 11.9.2003, edge.org/conversation/the-adjacent-possible) that he sees a possibility for four general laws (of biosphere?). One of these "laws" is the idea of adjacent possible.

"It may just be the case that biospheres on average keep expanding into the adjacent possible. By doing so they increase the diversity of what can happen next. It may be that biospheres, as a secular trend, maximize the rate of exploration of the adjacent possible. If they did it too fast, they would destroy their own internal organization, there may be internal gating mechanisms. This is why I call this an average secular trend, since they explore the adjacent possible as fast as they can get away with it."

Kauffman criticizes the concept of "work," based on Peter Atkins' book on the second law of thermodynamics. Atkins defines work as a thing, as a constrained release of energy, where many degrees of freedom are translated into a few.

For example, in a steam engine steam pushes on a piston and converts the randomness of the steam inside into a linear motion of the piston down the cylinder. The constraints in this case are the cylinder and the piston, the fact that piston is inside the cylinder, the fact that the two form a steam-tight seal, etc. But where did the constraints come from?

>"In virtually every case it takes work to make constraints. Somebody had to make the cylinder, somebody had to make the piston, and somebody had to assemble them.

>That it takes work to make constraints and it takes constraints to make work is a very interesting cycle. The idea is nowhere to be found in our definition of work, but it's physically correct in most cases, and certainly in organisms."

>"The life cycle of a cell is simply amazing. It does work to construct constraints on the release of energy, which does work to construct more constraints on the release of energy, which does work to construct even more constraints on the release of energy, and other kinds of work as well."

Another definition of adjacent possible: the set of specific and immediate possibilities available to an evolutionary system at any particular moment of its duration. (Gangle 2007:225, who also notes that a general tendency to expand the adjacent possible may characterize evolving systems as diverse as coral reefs and financial markets.)

Johnson (2010:33):
>"The history of life and human culture ... can be told as the story of a gradual but relentless probing of the adjacent possible, each new innovation opening up new paths to explore."

- the importance of adjacent possible is visible in the multiplicity (simultaneous innovation)
35:
>"Multiples have been invoked to support hazy theories about the "zeitgeist," but they have a much more grounded explanation. Good ideas are not conjured out of thin air; they are built out of a collection of existing parts, the composition of which expands (and, occasionally, contracts) over time. Some of those parts are conceptual: ways of solving problems, or new definitions of what constitutes a problem in the first place. Some of them are, literally, mechanical parts."
