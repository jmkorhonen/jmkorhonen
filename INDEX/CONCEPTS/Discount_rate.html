V:1987:EcologicalEconomics-Martinez-Alier :
Implied high discount rate because "future generations are better off" is perverse: it makes us less cautious towards the fates of future people, thus less likely that they will be better off (see also Risks:Existential )

Effects of discount rate on least cost Western European zero carbon energy system: p. 13, "With a lower discount rate of 3% [down from 8%, p. 6] nuclear capacity increases by 44% from 128 GW to 184 GW while PV capacity decreases by 61% from 502 GW to only 195 GW. The installed capacity of other technologies remains similar or decrease slightly compared to the capacities in the Reference scenario." However, note that p. 6, ref. 53 argues that using 8% discount rate for cost optimisation resulted in smaller deviations from the real world development of the UK electricity system, than a social discount rate of 3.5%.
V:2019:EnergScenarioDeepDecarbonization-van Zuijilen

E.g. V:2020:ClimatNetZeroEurope-McKinsey uses societal discount rate of 4% (p. 198); mentions that 3/4 of economists are comfortable with using low discount rate for climate analysis, median 2%

  • Stern uses 1.4% and suggests even 0% would be appropriate; Heritage Foundation suggests 7%
