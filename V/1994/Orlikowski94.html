Most discussion of social cognition does not specifically address technology per se, emphasizing instead strategy, innovation, or change management.

- Kaplan and Tripsas (2008) expanded the concept of technological frame from something that organizational actors share to a system of different frames - held by producers and users of technology as well as by institutional actors - that interact with each other

- SCOT literature and other sociological literature that examines collective cognitions and social constructions of technology (Bijker 1987, Bijker et al 1987; Henderson 1991; Sætnan 1991)
- in this literature, technology frames are the understanding that members of a social group come to have of particular technological artifacts, and they include not only knowledge about the particular technology but also local understanding of specific uses in a give setting.
- this local understanding - the contextual dimension - is important

Over time, the assumptions and categories of shared cognitive structures are often externalized and institutionalized (Giddens 1984; Hedberg et al., 1976, 1977; March and Simon 1958; Schein 1985). Once institutionalized, frames tend to produce cognitive inertia - a conservatism which may protect the organizational status quo, but which may also prevent an organization from adapting to a changing environment.
