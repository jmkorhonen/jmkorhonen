4

  • refers to Rockstrom et al's planetary boundaries hypothesis (2009) that has become a leading framework for thinking about global environmental problems
  • planetary boundaries hypothesis (PBH later) posits that there are nine hard, global biophysical limits to human development:
    • land-use change
    • biodiversity loss
    • nitrogen and phosphorous levels
    • freshwater use
    • ocean acidification
    • climate change
    • ozone depletion
    • aerosol loading
    • chemical pollution
  • PBH posits that crossing any of these boundaries may have catastrophic consequences for human welfare
  • PBH embraced by UN High-Level Panel on Global Sustainability and NGOs such as Oxfam and WWF
  • it is included in UNEP's Global Environmental Outlook 5, and underpins a reform proposal for global environmental institutions by the Earth Systems Governance Project
  • also proposed for 2012 Rio UN conference on sustainable development
  • this report reviews the empirical evidence for the planetary boundaries hypothesis, drawing upon an extensive literature review and informal peer review by leading experts

Key findings


  • 6 of 9 planetary boundaries do not have planetary biophysical thresholds in themselves: these are
    • land-use change
    • biodiversity loss
    • nitrogen levels
    • freshwater use
    • aerosol loading
    • chemical pollution
  • real, global biophysical threshol elements exist in the
    • global climate system
    • partly for ocean acidification (same driver as for climate, CO2)
    • ozone depletion (regional tipping points averted)
    • phosphorous levels (tipping point extremely far off)
  • for all remaining "boundaries" there are no global tipping points beyond which these ecological processes will begin to function in fundamentally different ways than they do at present or have historically
  • hence, setting the boundaries for these mechanisms is an arbitrary exercise

5

  • aside from their climate impacts, these non-threshold "boundaries" operate on local and regional, not global levels
  • there is little evidence to support the claim that transgressing any of the six non-threshold boundaries would have a net negative effect on human material welfare

Implications for science and policy


  • Global limits may risk misleading local and regional policy choices
    • for the 6 processes that lack global biophysical thresholds, limits or boundaries cannot be set with reference to science alone
    • changes in these systems necessarily entail continuous political and economic trade-offs between positive and negative impacts on human welfare, nearly all of which exist on local and regional, not global, scales.
    • Example 1: synthetic fertilizers boost production but can have significant local negative impacts and reduction may be warranted. In other places, notably in many parts of Africa, increased nitrogen use in agriculture would yield very significant benefits for human welfare, while limiting nitrogen use there would in no way mitigate the impacts of excessive nitrogen use in other regions - therefore, a global boundary is of little use to policy makers
    • Example 2: freshwater extraction can have damaging consequences in areas of high population density and/or freshwater use, while in other areas, moderate increases would not endanger ecosystem health. Limiting human use of water in one river basin or aquifer does not ameliorate water scarcity elsewhere, thus making a global limit meaningless.

7

  • ecological degradation has thus far shown little correlation with global measures of human material welfare
    • PBH rests on an assumption that environmental variables are closely linked to human welfare, and that, consequently, loss of ecosystem services or natural capital implies declining human welfare

  • with the exception of climate, there is little reason to assume that other conditions that characterized the Holocene are particularly important to human material welfare
    • PBH assumes that the Holocene epoch represents the most desirable state of the environment for human welfare
    • there are very good reasons to prefer the climate of Holocene, but there is little evidence that land cover, nitrogen levels, biodiversity, or any of the other non-climate systems had in themselves a stability or level that was particularly beneficial for human development
    • in many ways, the human population and the level of material welfare that exist today fundamentally depend on the fact that some of the non-climate systems DO NOT remain at Holocene levels.
      • Nitrogen and increased freshwater withdrawals for irrigation were of critical importance to the enormous increase in food production
      • Land-use change has been fundamental to expanding agriculture and thus feeding the world
      • Until now, the net benefit in terms of human welfare of using fossil fuels and thus emitting carbon dioxide has been immense

  • the relationship between human material welfare and ecological systems is better explicated through trade-offs than boundaries
    • there are multiple costs and benefits of human impacts in the environment, and balancing these is an inherently political question - science alone cannot resolve it

10

  • the novelty of the planetary boundaries framework and its central justification stems from its foundation in complex systems theory, or "resilience thinking"
  • that is, assuming that linear changes in a complex system may accumulate to produce non-linear or even exponential changes
  • under these conditions, environmental management cannot be based on strictly linear dynamics, but instead has to adopt as its main priority the avoidance of tipping points

14

  • e.g. in land use changes, one set of ecosystem services has been replaced with another (food)
  • this is not inherently negative

15

  • there is a subjective preference for pristine nature
  • the authors do not answer the question why the Holocene baseline is superior
  • there is no evidence that land-use change at the Holocene baseline corresponds to the optimal state from a human welfare perspective

17

  • ecologists agree that at local sites and on average, greater plant diversity leads to greater productivity in plant communities, greater nutrient retention in ecosystems and greater ecosystem stability
    • evidence, however, tends to come from very small-scale or laboratory studies
  • a commonplace reasoning suggests that global biodiversity loss therefore leads to a weakening of global ecosystems
  • but this chain of reasoning is undermined by the evidence that global biodiversity loss has not in fact been paralleled by losses on local and regional scales, which is where ecosystem dynamics chiefly operate (note 37, 38)
    • in an analysis of over 1000 field studies, Vila et al showed that on average, "abundance and diversity of the resident species decreased in invaded sites, whereas primary production and several ecosystem processes were enhanced."
  • this may even suggest that on shorter timescales and averaged out over many places, the current trend of global homogenization and local increases in species richness may maintain or even enhance ecosystem processes, which is the exact opposite of what is generally predicted

18

  • extinction rates are not that alarming: likely extinction rate only 10x that of background, and that's when island species are included
  • assuming higher extinction rates requires an assumption of "extinction debt" that will take decades or centuries to play out
  • if we assume that all species that are currently critically endangered would go extinct over a 500-year period, it would take up to 10,000 years before it would qualify as a mass extinction
  • of continental species, only six birds and three mammals were recorded as going extinct since 1500
    • this corresponds to extinction rates for mammals and birds of 0.89-7.4 times and 0.60-5.9 times the background rate
  • Barnosky et al. calculate that "if extinction were limited to "critically endangered" species over the next century and those extinction rates continued, the time until 75% of species were lost per group would be 890 years for amphibians, 2265 years for birds, and 1519 years for mammals" (note 44)
  • mass extinction is agreed to mean 75% species loss
