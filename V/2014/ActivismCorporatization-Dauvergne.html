loc49
More and more activists, especially those toiling inside large advocacy organizations, are ... speaking in market-friendly language. They are calling for a gentler capitalism - for fair trade, for certification, for eco-markets.

...

Solutions to global problems involve campaigns for ethical purchasing: to brand social causes and sell feelings of "doing good" to the "cappuccino class."

Without a doubt most activists still want to speak truth to power. But nowadays they are entangled in this power.
- partnerships with big-brand companies

loc83

Business is seeking out advocacy organizations for legitimacy and marketing opportunities. But activists are courting companies for funds and partnerships with as much, if not more, enthusiasm.

...

One consequence for world politics is that activism is now less "radical" than it was forty or fifty years ago, at least in terms of demanding systemic and far-reaching change. Another consequence, as we document in chapter 2, is that, with each passing year, activist fundraising, projects, and goals are becoming more entwined with corporate interests. Unraveling corporatism from activism is getting progressively harder.

loc100
Those NGOs striving to reform capitalist institutions seem especially prone to corporatization. At first this finding may seem counterintuitive. Yet, in many ways, it is perfectly logical given the power of capitalism to assimilate criticism and dissent. Multinational corporations are keen to partner with large, global NGOs in particular, not only to mold the nature of criticism and pressure but also to legitimize business growth, gain efficiencies and competitive advantages, and earn profits.

loc185

Anti-corporate activism certainly continues in many places ... But signs of the corporatization of activism far outweigh signs of anti-corporate activity.

loc190
Another sign of the corporatization of activism is the growth of corporate-style "fundraising," where, although it may be admirable, the "cause" has come to be defined by branding and advertising.

loc300
- social bonds are weaker and working-class politics no longer arise out of neighborhood, ethnic or religious groups, or other pre-existing associations

Far more people today participate in collective action as individuals to advocate for a common cause ... Many of these groups do not have strong community or historical bonds of faith, trust, or solidarity. This has lasting consequences for the change capacity of activist movements, especially those relying on more impersonal commitments...

- previously groups formed first, then they found politics; now, politics comes first and creates the group
- without pre-existing social bonds, politics alone usually do not generate a lasting or robust connectivity

loc322

Greenpeace

- started in 1970 as anti-nuclear weapons organization
- in 1979 Greenpeace International set up in Amsterdam
- HQ controls its brand image, Greenpeace is a registered trade mark
- worldwide, thousands of employees, around 3 million financial "supporters" and 28 national and regional branches operating in more than 40 countries.
Greenpeace International reported income of more than €60 million in 2011

Al Jazeera World documentary 2012: Greenpeace: From hippies to lobbyists captures well the trend over the last four decades

The history of Greenpeace is typical among international environmental NGOs

WWF:

in 2012 had an operating income of €593 million, spending more than €105 million on fundraising alone

loc357
- Greenpeace considered less corporatized than many activist organizations, because it continues to criticize corporations, and does not accept money from governments, political parties, or corporations

loc359
But the scope of what Greenpeace is calling a "victory" is nonetheless instructive of how deep the process of institutionalization is reaching: for example, one of its big victories in 2011 was getting Mattel to change its Barbie doll packaging, which Greenpeace linked to tropical deforestation. Moreover, Greenpeace, once one of the most anti-corporate NGOs, is now cooperating and partnering with big-brand companies, such as with Coca-Cola, PepsiCo, and Unilever to develop "natural refrigerants" to reduce greenhouse gas emissions from food and drink retailing. "Corporations," Greenpeace USA acknowledges, "can be extraordinarily dynamic, powerful, and swift allies."

loc372
Christine MacDonald, a former employee of Conservation International and author of Green, Inc., deplores such partnerships: "Not only do the largest conservation groups take money from companies deeply implicated in environmental crimes, they have become something like satellite PR offices for the corporations that support them."

loc442
- defines "radical activist" as one who challenges political and corporate authority and calls for structural change to alter the outcomes of markets and politics. Solutions for them cannot arise from within the structures of the capitalist system but must instead get at the root causes.
- these kinds of radicals are marginalized and assimilated by corporatization of activism, and these are also activists that states are particularly keen to track and jail.

Big oil and gas
- between 2007 and 2010 Sierra Club got more than $25 million from Chesapeake Energy, one of the world's largest gas drillers and a promoter of fracking

- BP also donated millions of $ to Nature Conservancy
- many critics believe NC responded lamely to the BP oil spill in the Gulf of Mexico in 2010
