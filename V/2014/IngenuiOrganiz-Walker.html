Organizational ingenuity encompasses innovative activity within institutional constraints (Lampel, Honig and Drori 2011). Given the role of institutional constraints, we draw from institutional
614
theory to orient our research on this emerging construct in organization studies (Suddaby, 2006).

  • connects ingenuity to two related puzzles in institutional theory concerning how stakeholders interact with the institutional environment:
    • 1. the tension between top-down and bottom-up processes in the creation, maintenance, and change of institutions
    • 2. the paradox of embedded agency: how institutional change is possible, given that stakeholders are constrained by the very institutions they seek to change (Garud et al. 2007, Seo & Creed, 2002; Zietsma and Lawrence, 2010).
  • in both puzzles, existing institutional constraints place top-down pressure on stakeholders to conform, a process known as institutional isomorphism (DiMaggio and Powell, 1983)
  • but stakeholders often seek to change these constraints from the bottom-up
  • Organizing to change institutions is a component of institutional entrepreneurship, but ingenuity is unique because it also includes entrepreneurial innovation within structural constraints (Lampel et al., 2011)
  • little empirical attention to how stakeholders in embryonic industries use bottom-up processes when facing powerful institutional constraints

Theoretical Foundations


Ingenuity

  • little research conceptualizing ingenuity exists
  • adopts the definition presented by the editors of this special issue: ingenuity is "the ability to create innovative solutions within structural constraints using limited resources and imaginative problem solving" (Lampel et al., 2011, p. 458)

615

Ingenuity takes place within institutional constraints, and it involves adapting to existing constraints, developing new domains of action, and/or changing the constraints (Lampel et al., 2011)

  • closely aligned but yet different from creativity, innovation and agility
  • Creativity: defined as "the production of novel and useful ideas in any domain"
  • Innovation: the "successful implementation of creative ideas within an organization" (both from Amabile et al., 1996:1155)
  • Agility: "firm's ability to cope with rapid relentless and uncertain changes and thrive in a competitive environment of continually and unpredictably changing opportunities" (Lu and Ramamurthy 2011:932)
    • ingenuity bears some resemblance to strategic agility, but agility focuses less on particular ideas and more on adapting to a changing, uncertain, and competitive environment.
    • ingenuity focuses more on ideas implemented in the context of constraining institutional environment, which may or may not be changing (Lampel et al., 2011)

What sets innovation apart from creativity is its implementation within organizations. Creativity can thus be viewed as a first step to innovation. Innovative ideas need not be new to the world but must be perceived as new to the people and can include new products, processes, markets, supplies and organizations (Rogers, 1983; Schumpeter, 1986; Van de Ven, 1986). Ingenuity is "the ability to create innovative solutions," and it incorporates not only producing novel, useful ideas but also implementing them. Therefore, creativity and innovation are necessary - but not sufficient - parts of ingenuity. The final parts of ingenuity are the presence of structural constraints and stakeholders with limited resources innovating within these constraints.

The paradox of embedded agency in institutional theory


  • Authors define institutional constraints as limitations or restrictions on the behavior of stakeholders (Seo and Creed, 2002; Scott, 2008)
  • initial research on institutional theory conceived stakeholders as relatively passive recipients of constraints (DiMaggio and Powell 1983; Mexias, 1990)
  • subsequent research conceived stakeholders as actively pursuing interests and thus having varying responses to institutional pressures
  • potential change agents can mobilize and exhibit collective action to change institutions

These two areas of investigation have been labeled the paradox of embedded agency where stakeholders are constrained in their behaviors by institutions, yet they also influence and change institutions (Garud et al., 2007; Seo and Creed, 2002)

  • authors propose that ingenuity is important to the bottom-up efforts of stakeholders in the paradox of embedded agency

616

  • importance of legitimacy for stakeholders who face institutional constraints
  • Legitimacy = "the generalized acceptability of an entity or practice within a social system" (Deephouse and Suchman, 2008; Suchman, 1995)
  • legitimacy important because it enables an entity to obtain necessary resources, reduce uncertainty, and ultimately enhance survivability (Aldrich and Fiol, 1994; DiMaggio and Powell, 1983; Drori and Honig, 2013: Suchman, 1995)

Because ingenuity occurs within institutional constraints it is important to consider the legitimacy of ingenious stakeholders and their actions.

Examines four types of legitimacy in the study:

  • pragmatic: what's in it for me? (self-interest)
  • moral: what's in it for society? (based on normative approval and the promotion of social welfare)
  • cognitive: passive acceptance and the absence of questions (Meyer and Scott, 1983; Suchman, 1995)
  • regulative: acceptability to the rules and procedures of authoritative bodies, most commonly the state: top-down institutional constraint

Ingenuity strategies


Constraint-challenging multi-stakeholder collaborations. Stakeholders tried to drive change through the formation of collaborations; challenged the rationality of two identified institutional constraints. Ultimately not very successful.

626

Constraint-escaping new product and market development. When the first ingenuity strategy failed, a second strategy emerged.

  • internal transitions within individual firms were witnessed, as they recognized both the severity of the constraints and their inability to alter them
  • companies developed a variety of products that functioned independent of grid access, e.g. solar kiosk/bar, solar-powered golf carts, solar cooker...
  • constrained local market also made entrepreneurs to consider export opportunities
  • meanwhile, the first ingenuity strategy was being continued

627

Constraint-complying new product partnerships. Development of new product partnerships that found niches within the Ontario power grid. Partners acquiesced to the institutional constraints while "consciously and strategically" complying in anticipation of "self-serving benefits". But here, instead of challenging constraints together, the partners collaborated while complying with constraints.

Constraint-escaping new product partnerships. Partnerships developed new products that enabled firms to escape the constraints.
