Weiss, M., Hoegl, M., & Gibbert, M. (2014). Perceptions of Material Resources in Innovation Projects: What Shapes Them and How Do They Matter? Journal of Product Innovation Management, 31(2), 278â€“291. http://doi.org/10.1111/jpim.12095


- prior results from studies on the role of material resources for team performance in innovation projects
- focuses on team members' perceptions of the provided material resources' adequacy
- surveys data from 121 innovation projects in the electronics industry
- measured two outcome dimensions of innovation project performance: degree of new product quality and new product novelty
- team potency and workload are identified as socio-cognitive drivers of innovation project teams' perceptions of material resource adequacy
- perceived material resource adequacy relates positively to new product quality, while it relates negatively to new product novelty
- results provide evidence that socio-cognitive factors have significant influence on perceptrions on the team, not just on the individual level
- the effects of perceived material resource adequacy differ depending on the indicators used for measuring the outcomes of an innovation project

Previous research examining the role of material resources in innovation projects did not take into consideration project endogenous factors that could influence the perception of material resource adequacy (De Dreu 2006; Hoegl and Parboteeah, 2006)

- turns to findings of grounded cognition (Barsalou, 2008) focusing on the role of simulation in human cognition (e.g. Decety and Grezes 2006; Kent and Laberts, 2008).
- these approaches point to the mechanism in cognitive processing that when evaluating or judging tasks, actions, or objects, individuals execute mental simulations of these tasks, actions, or the actions connected with these objects in order to predict their outcomes or their favorability (Barsalou, 2008; Decety and Grezes, 2006).

- Witt and Proffitt (2008): first factor personal performance capabilities, i.e. the assessment of the possibility to perform a certain action satisfactorily
- there is evidence that personal performance capabilities include the perception of size (Wesp et al. 2004), distance (Lessard et al, 2009) and slope (Proffitt et al. 2001)
- mechanism behind this observation seems to be that individuals mentally simulate how well they are able to perform a certain action (Witt and Proffitt, 2008)

In innovation teams, team members likely perform similar simulations, eventually leading to a judgment of the team's capability to accomplish a certain task. Team members' judgment is either expressed verbally in the team or through a more implicitly shared opiinion developed through nonverbal interaction (Campion, Mesker, and Higgs, 1993).

- high workload is likely to influence perception of material resource adequacy, just as a heavy backpack makes the mountain appear steeper
- time pressure has been indicated to influence perception of material resource adequacy (Amabile et al. 2002)

- prior to this paper, virtually no empirical work had systematically investigated how material resources actually impact the performance of innovation project teams

- most studies where the relationship between material resource adequacy and innovation project outcomes is incorporated in the analyses reveal no significant relationships (e.g. Cummings 2004; De Dreu 2006; Hoegl and Parboteeah 2006; Joshi and Sharma 2004; Leenders and Voermans 2007; Sethi, Smith and Park, 2001)
- however, in these studies it had not been the main focus and in most cases, resource adequacy has been a control variable
- only two empirical studies yielded significant results, but contradict each other: Song and Noh (2006) found more adequate material resources leading to better outcomes; Sethi and Iqbal (2008) discovered relatively higher material resource provisions to innovation projects to be significantly negatively related to the success of newly developed products

- IMPORTANT TO DISTINGUISH BETWEEN MORE SPECIFIC OUTCOME VARIABLES when building an appropriately fine-grained theoretical foundation for analyzing the role of material resource adequacy
