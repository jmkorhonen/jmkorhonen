Trust in social psychology


trust implies allowing oneself to be in a potentially vulnerable position relative to another, while possessing some knowledge of the other that inspires trust in his goodwill, i.e. his good intentions (see e.g. Luhmann 1979)

Most researchers see trust as a function of imperfect information (Lewis and Weigert 1985, Oakes 1990)

Granovetter (1992:39) quotes from Simmel: "the person who knows completely need not trust; while the person who knows nothing, can on no rational grounds afford even confidence

  • in total ignorance it is only possible to have faith and/or gamble
  • under perfect information, there is no trust but merely rational calculation

  • trust makes sense only in interpersonal relationships

  • trust tends to evoke trust, and distrust distrust
  • as trust erodes, the things causing distrust might first be considered as accidental incidents, but after repeated evidence of intentionality distrust takes over (see Fox 1975, Zucker 1986)
  • trust is usually situation specific (Frank 1988)
  • Moorman et al (!993): both belief and behavioral intention must be present for trust to exist
    • trust is limited if one only believes in the trustworthiness of the other, but is not willing to rely on it
    • and if someone is willing to rely on another party without believing in that person's trustworthiness, this reliance may be more a function of power and control than of trust

Trust in philosophy


  • moral philosophers have written very little about trust
  • Plato and Aristotle only indirectly implied trust

  • reliance is different and more limited than trust

Trust in economics


  • Economists have not traditionally paid much attention to the role of trust in market exchange (Lorenz 1988)
    • neo-classical ideal market has perfect information and pure competition between independent and faceless traders; no need for trust as competitive market is supposed to control any deception
    • rational choice theory excludes differences among actors: no one is more or less trustworthy than others (see the discussion in Noorderhaven 1992)
  • in imperfect markets where a small number of traders build long-term relationships and make relation-specific investments drew attention to trust
  • in economics, trust is often regarded as mutual confidence: "implicit contracting", i.e. an individual or a firm trusts another to do what it has promised to do (Zucker 1986)
  • Economists believe that repeated games like Prisoner's Dilemma (Axelrod 1984) have demonstrated the effects of learning, communication and the "shadow-of-the-future": the participants will cooperate when it pays them to do so, i.e. if it is rational
  • trust is seen as a response to expected future behavior

275

  • some economists think trust is a positive externality

Trust and contract law


  • legal enforcement can be slow, expensive and inconvenient
  • the morality-based legal doctrines and ethical principles are emphasized especially in relational exchange (see Macneil 1980)

276

  • Young and Wilkinson (1989) found that firms whose contracts with other firms were formal had more conflicts
  • they suggest trust is most likely to grow in situations where it is given the option to develop naturally
