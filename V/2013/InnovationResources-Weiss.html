Weiss, M., Hoegl, M., & Gibbert, M. (2013). The Influence of Material Resources on Innovation Project Outcomes. R&D Management, 43(2), 151–161.

INDEX:Innovation , INDEX:SCARCITIES

- builds on a concept of innovation project team's *resource elasticity* to help disentangle the influence of material resource availability on innovation projects
- concept analogous to price elasticity
- in resource-elastic teams, the relationship between material resources and innovation outcomes is positive
- resource-inelastic teams show no or even a negative relationship between material resource adequacy and team performance
- neither adequate nor inadequate material resources seem to be a reliable predictor of success
- success factor is therefore seen to be team's key characteristics

Shostack 1988, Woods et al, 2009 as examples of innovation success achieved because of constrained material resources

- less than adequate material resources may stimulate and facilitate innovation, but they do not necessarily do so

- resource elasticity originally a concept in Hoegl et al 2008

- defines innovation project performance according to nonmarket-dependent outcomes of the innovation project: product quality, product innovativeness, and project efficiency (in terms of cost and time)
- material resources refer to liquid funding available to the innovation project team, i.e. budget, as well as monetary-equivalent resources such as technical equipment and prototype testing, but not team staff
- "adequacy" of material resources is defined when they are sufficient to achieve the given objectives of an innovation project (Hoegl et al. 2008), and constrained when they are not (Mone et al, 1998)

"commonly held assumption that resources are a key determinant of innovation project performance:" Cyert and March (1963), Damanpour (1991), Amabile et al. (1996), Gerchak, (1998), Messica and David (2000), Amabile and Kramer (2011)

- teams Weiss et al. term "resource victims" "will lament, and be stifled by, perceptions of inadequate material resources and eventually surrender to this situation..."
- become self-declared sufferers of inadequate material resources

- uses example of Gibbert and Scranton (2009) as another example of resource scarcity-driven ingenuity

- other examples: Michael Dell was forced to build custom computers on order has he lacked money and storage space for inventory; invented new business model (Gibbert and Välikangas 2004)
- Dutch flower industry was forced to innovate wherever possible in the value chain, coming up with creative solutions that counter the given natural disadvantages

- agrees that resource adequacy is about PERCEPTIONS and individual cognitive comparison processes

- Kor et al. (2007) examine differences in how adequate people perceive available material resources to be

157

...a team's perception of material resource adequacy is expected to depend heavily on socio-cognitive aspects.

- Weiss et al (2013) illustrated that perceptions of resource adequacy actually influence innovation performance
- "half full" or "half empty" type behaviors
- studies in cognitive psychology show that perceptions are prone to influences not necessarily connected to the aspects being evaluated (Witt and Proffitt 2008; Ackerman et al, 2010; Clarkson et al, 2010; Jostmann et al., 2009 (showed that individuals' experience of heavy weight increased evaluations of monetary value))
- results from field of cognitive psychology emphasize that perceptions play a key role in explaining task behavior and the resulting success
- Clarkson et al (2010) found that a person's perceived depletion may influence subsequent task performance regardless of the actual state of depletion
