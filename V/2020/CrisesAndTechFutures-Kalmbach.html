273

German historian Reinhart Koselleck's classic study Vergangene Zukunft "points out that the historical era we know as "modernity" - an era marked by secularization as well as universalization of the concept of crisis - began at the moment in time when people started to think of the future as a "time-space" that they THEMSELVES HAD THE POWER TO SHAPE AND RESHAPE PROACTIVELY THROUGH THEIR OWN ACTIONS AND CHOICES."
- Koselleck however did not further unpack the black box of technology

Jasanoff and Kim, SOCIO-TECHNICAL IMAGINARIES, defined as "collectively imagined forms of social life and social orders connected to - and reflected in - the design and fulfilment of nation-specific scientific and/or technological projects."
274
- Jasanoff and Kim emphasize the strong "futuristic" element of these imaginaries: visions of "desirable futures" as well as fears of failures and "unintended harm" that are inscribed into things and technologies. (Jasanoff and Kim, Containing the Atom, 120)

- Jens Beckert (Imagined Futures, p. 169) has pointed out that "utopian visions of a pretended future reality - imagined futures - are an impetus for innovative activity."

- this article argues that FEARS and DYSTOPIAN VISIONS that are fueled by narratives of past, present, and future crises are also an important impetus for creative activity

Fridlund and history of people's experiences of imagined futures



- Mats Fridlund has recently pleaded for a new kind of technological history that looks into "people's experiences of their imagined futures" (Fridlund: Buckets, Bollards and Bombs, 397) PPL:FRIDLUND_Mats

Transnational perspective crucial



- transnational perspective is crucial: threats are seen to be transnational as well as the technologies

- fears have strongly impacted research agendas and resulted in the development of new technologies
- however, social sciences and humanities research has mainly focused on people's fears OF technologies
- the question how fears drive technological development has received almost no attention
- historical research on nuclear technology is a telling example: nuclear fears are a well-researched topic (Weart, Nuclear Fear; Kalmbach, Revisiting the Nuclear Age)
277
- but the fears that drove the development of nuclear technology had been left out
- likewise, in the discussion about genetically modified crops, we seldom read about the fear of a growing world population being the driving force behind the development of this technology

- history of emotions has become a flourishing field of research; however, it has not yet connected with research into history of technology, and history of technology has not paid too much attention to emotions

- research on how fears get manifested in technological artifacts: allows historians to decode technologies as a means to nurture, perpetuate, and handle fears

- sociologists proclaim we are living in an "age of fear"

278

Techno-Fixes



In a proclaimed crisis situations, fears related to past events and experiences are projected onto the future, and "technological fixes" are developed in order to prevent these fearful futures from becoming a tangible - and terrifying - reality.

(For an insightful study of the concept of technofixes, see Sean F. Johnston, "Alvin Weinberg and the Promotion of the Technological Fix")

Scarcity


- looks at space mining

"...fear of resource scarcity has deep historical roots and has been shown to only rarely be connected to actual physical scarcity but instead to systemic issues of economic, social, and technological kinds. (Vikström, Hanna. The Specter of Scarcity)

- in parallel to this narrative of fear, there is an overarching narrative of resource exploitation, as the base of constant economic growth, which further legitimizes the idea that space is the next rational frontier for our resource-thirsty societies
- fear is thus a crucial emotional aspect driving these narratives

"During periods of insecurity, historically contingent crisis imaginaries tend to evolve, linking developments in the historical present to cultural memories of a fearful past and visions of an unwanted future."

"Thus, a historical understanding of these imaginaries, along with their societal and material aftermath - including their impact in relation to political hcoice and decision-making - is imperative for the history of technology."

"This calls for thorough historical analyses of the enganglements of technology with the cultural, imaginary stuff of crises;
