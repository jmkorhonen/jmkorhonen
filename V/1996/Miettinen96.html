Explores the relevance of four theories of invention in the context of a Finnish industrial innovation process

  1. The traditional genius theory
  2. The classical sociological theory of cultural maturation
  3. The theory of attribution
  4. The theory of chance and serendipity

Uses enzyme-aided pulp bleaching as empirical case; a research-based innovation. Focus on four problems:

- is it possible to find an individual inventor in this process?
- what was the significance of shared, global cultural resources and practices in the invention process?
- how was the status of invention attributed to the experimental results?
- What was the significance of chance and contingency in the process?

## Genius theory

- criticizes genius theory strongly, notes that recent (in 1996) empirical research has proposed "systemic accounts" of creativity in which the sociocultural surroundings must be taken into consideration; references from 1989/90

## Cultural maturation

- multiple discoveries and the theory of cultural maturation: research initiated in 1922 Ogburn and Thomas paper "Are inventions inevitable?" where they presented a list of 148 multiple independent discoveries

- they proposed two essential factors: cultural preparation and the development of scientific technique and instrumentation.
- Merton (1961) thought that the study of multiple discoveries could be a "strategic object" for science studies
- Merton (1973:371):

>"Such occurrences suggest that discoveries become virtually inevitable when prequisite kinds of knowledge and tools accumulate in man's cultural store and when attention of an appreciable number of investigations becomes focused on a problem by emerging social needs, by development internal to the science, or by both."

- Merton believed that joint problems, joint scientific theories and joint instruments are the essential ingredients of cultural maturation and at the same time preconditions of an invention
- Hughes's reverse salient is a development of Merton's common or universal problem, and Constant regarded "functional failure" as a major impetus to technical change
- Constant (1984:648): All functional failure based problems or anomalies are objective: a specific system really does not work well in a specific context.

NOTE: from this functional failure it does not necessarily follow that the solutions are objective, and it could be argued that if functional failures need to be objective, then there would be very few true functional failures at all

IDEA: When does a technology fail? When a functional failure occurs? Outokumpu case... how it's far more complicated than that

- Weingart (1984:132) regards the identification of the weakest point or critical problem as the core of invention:

>"One of the mechanisms of change, the essence of inventive activity, is the improvement of "unsatisfactory patterns," the rectification of "imbalances" or the identification of the "weakest point.""
- corresponds to the conception according to which the formulation of a problem or question in important inventions has been more essential than its solution, which is more a matter of work

- Rosenberg (1963) conceptualized the relationship between the global and local aspects of technological problems; the concept of technological convergence
- the common nature of problems in a wide range of disparate and apparently unrelated industries means that once they are solved locally, the solution can be diffused across the whole industry.
##Attribution theory

- Social constructivists have criticized the cultural preparation theory for its universalistic and deterministic bias.
- attribution theory questions the very quality of originality or inventiveness: these are labels attributed to a product by communities of specialists or users - in many cases after the construction has already been established (Brannigan 1981; Schaffer, 1994).
##Theory of serendipidity

- argues this theory has persisted especially in engineering literature and in the folklore tradition of chemistry (Haensel, 1994; Roth-Bernstein, 1994; Royston 1989)
