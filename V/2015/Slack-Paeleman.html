- there is a classic resource-based theory literature ("more is better") and resource constraints literature ("less is more" perspective)

- classic RBV literature depicts firms as heterogeneous bundles of resources, and stresses the importance of slack resources (or lack thereof) rather than the absolute amount of resources possessed by firms
- particular attention devoted to the characteristics of resources that lead to competitive advantage
- it is argued that firms with more valuable resources than other firms will exhibit superior performance
- resource constraints theorists argue that value can be destroyed by adding more resources to a firm's resource pool (Moskowski, 2002)
- especially in an entrepreneurial setting, resource constraints drive entrepreneurs to work more efficiently and foster entrepreneurial ingenuity, which ultimately benefits a firm's performance and survival prospects (Baker and Nelson, 2005)

Resource constraints theorists posit that there are important costs associated with large resource endowments, such as core rigidities and reduced experimentation, which impair an entrepreneur's ability to perceive and act upon opportunities (Mosakowski, 2002).

When firms have abundant slack resources and do not experience constraints, entrepreneurs are likely to feel comfortable with the status quo and are less likely to take strategic actions (e.g., Mosakowski, 2002). Debruyne et al. (2010), for instance, show that abundant resources lead people to believe that they are able to react effectively to competitive attacks but at the same time make them less motivated to take actions to counter such attacks.

- resource abundance tends to lead to attitude that the firm is doing well (Danneels, 2008)
- this attitude in turn leads to a more complacent, inward-looking and risk-averse behavior
- people have a natural tendency to engage in exploitation at the expense of exploration (Levinthal and March, 1993)
- firms characterized by resource abundance are expecetd to focus their attention more on exploiting and leveraging their existing resource bundles to generate profits (Agarwal and Audretsch, 2001; Kraatz and Zajac, 2001)
- Bhide (1992) argues that startups that acquire significant amounts of external resources are confronted with a serious challenge because external stakeholders often push firms to exploit their existing strategies - for which they raised those resources - but this can hinder entrepreneurs from following the try-it, fix-it approach required in the uncertain environments in which start-ups flourish
- resource abundant firms are more likely to focus on "static efficiency" by applying and improving their current resource bundles at the expense of their "dynamic efficiency" from pursuing

- firms that experience resource constraints are forced to seek alternative ways to create profits
- will therefore engage more often in exploration and more radical business opportunities (Mosakowski, 2002)
- furthermore, resource-scarce firms are more likely to leverage their existing resources more efficiently (Baker and Nelson, 2005; see also Hamel and Prahalad 1993, example on Dell & mail order sales)
- Starr and Macmillan (1990) show how resource-constrained entrepreneurs rely on resource cooptation strategies, such as obtaining access to resources that are underutilized by others through borrowing, begging, or scavenging
- "bounded creativity" mechanism argues human mind is more productive when some restrictions apply (Goldenberg et al. 2001)

- multiple constraints may become insurmountable (Hoegl et al., 2008)
