The concept that mineral resources are finite and irreplaceable is relatively modern, probably expressed for the first time by Georg Bauer (also known as Agricola) in the sixteenth century. In a more general sense, the idea that all natural resources are finite seems to have appeared for the first time in economics with the work of Thomas Malthus

...

In time, the attitude of the mainstream economics thought seems to have evolved in the direction of maintaining that the physical limits to the amount of mineral deposits are either unimportant for the foreseeable future, or that they could be always circumvented by substitution or other methods.

13

  • Limits to Growth described the depletion process correctly: it assumed that the problem is not the finiteness of the amount of each element in the Earth's crust, but in the gradual dissipation of the energy potentials of the mineral deposits.
  • Mineral deposits embed enormous amounts of energy, a condition that has allowed humankind to separate and collect the pure elements from their ores at a relatively low energy cost
  • energy cost of the production of mineral commodities increases with time
    • eventually, there will not exist any exploitable mineral ores
    • earth's crust may reach the condition defined as "Thanatia" in the book by the same title by Valero in 2014
    • "dead" earth from a mineral viewpoint: a planet where the energy cost needed to collect and separate elements from the undifferentiated crust in the amounts that are typical of the present industrial system is so high that it is unthinkable to do so, unless it were possible for humankind to deploy flows of energy that today are unimaginable.

  • average total flow of solar energy that reaches Earth's sufrace is estimated as 89 000 TWh or 87 000 TWh
  • Fraction of this is processed by the biosphere;
    • gross planetary production (GPP) is estimated as 105-177 Pg of carbon per year
    • as reducing six moles of CO2 to one mole of hexose requires approximately 9450 kJ, we can calculate that the total energy generated by photosynthetic organisms on planet Earth in the form of organic compounds is approximately 500-900 TW
      • translates to average efficiency of biosphere as an energy transduction mechanism as 0.8 %; some studies report lower, 0.3 %
        • taking 0.5% as the average, we arrive to a total energy produced by photosynthesis of approximately 400 TW
        • however, on ideal conditions photosynthesis is much more efficient and crops and vegetated areas can reach a photosynthesis efficiency in the range of 2 to 4 percent
