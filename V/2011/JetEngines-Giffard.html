“The well-known German lead in jet production and deployment was not the natural result of the country’s technical lead, nor of the new aero-engine’s superiority to existing, out-numbered piston-engines, but rather of the country’s desperate position at the end of the war. The jet engines that Germany produced were much easier to build than existing piston engines, requiring only a fraction of the man-hours and material. They were not wonder weapons, but ersatz ones.”

“…large-scale manufacture of engines of desperation.”

17

“…jet engines took about five years to develop to the point of first production, about the same amount of time as the gestation of a new piston engine, contrary to optimistic, early hopes in industry and government.”

“One of the reasons that the aero-engine industry was able to take up the new type of engine so effectively was because the new engines could be designed by combining independent elements — many of which the industry was already familiar with.”

19

“…unlike Germany later in the war, Britain deemphasised production in favour of a broad development programme. By the end of the war, despite having produced a smaller number of production engines than the Germans, Britain boasted both the most developed jet engines and the most varied jet engine programme in the world. This encompassed all of the country’s aero-engine makers and included the work of Rolls-Royce, which led the world market in large jet engines after the war. So it was that, in contrast to the German V1 and V2 that spawned post-war American military missile and rocket projects, American aero-engine firms did not in the post-war period turn to confiscated Herman jet engines as a basis for future development but instead licensed British jet engines with wartime heritages.”

20

“The point of telling the story of the jet engine with these key inventive institutions at the end is to understand invention not as the precursor to development and production, as it is usually treated, but much more broadly as an extended activity in the context of development and production, constantly impacted by development and production decisions. Novelty did not appear through the work of unique inventors in rarefied places, but was carried out in the same kinds of places as and through the work of the same kinds of people who were engaged in development and production.”

Interwar literature on innovation
- formal study of invention in the 20th century inaugurated most famously by a group of American sociologists
- “sociology of invention”
- Gilfillan, of Ogburn school,
- Ogburn’s school studied invention as a way to explain a central sociological problem of the day: the observed differences between cultures
- rejected then-popular racialist-biological explanations for cultural differences
- argued instead that humanity had common intellectual capacities
- this led them to favour a determinist theory of invention, in which invention occurred everywhere through the combination of elements from a shared cultural base

(More subtly than the ‘cultural lag’ thesis for which he is more generally known, Ogburn argued both that culture influenced invention and that invention influenced culture. (McGee, ‘Making up Mind,’ 773-801.))

- the key piece of evidence: things were commonly invented by multiple people
- William Ogburn and Dorothy Thomas argued in a 1922 paper that the frequent existence of “duplicate inventions or discoveries”, which they empirically illustrated with 148 examples from a range of fields, suggested that invention must “be explained in cultural and social terms, that is, in terms of sociology and not in terms of biology and psyhology.” (William Ogburn and Dorothy Thomas, ‘Are Inventions Inevitable? A Note on Social Evolution’, Political Science Quarterly 37, no. 1 (1922): 83-98.)

(Andrew Jamison, ‘Technology’s Theorists: Conceptions of Innovation in Relation to Science and Technology Policy’, Technology and Culture 30, no. 3 (1989): 505-533.) MacLeod has discussed the work of nineteenth-century theorists. (Christine MacLeod, Heroes of Invention: Technology, Liberalism and British Identity, 1750-1914 (Cambridge: CUP, 2008).)

For Gilfillan, the crucial factors were the inventor’s foresight and his social context rather than location or means.

30

- Another interwar history of invention: Usher’s A History of Mechanical Invention (first published in 1929)
- studied invention in order to address the general problem of historical change
- used history of mechanical invention to “securely” establish “many of the primary features of the process” of “the emergence of novelty in thought and action.” Mechanical invention was “broadly representative of every feature of the general process of invention… [and] admits of a more rigorous historical treatment than is possible in many portions of the history of philosophy and the arts.” [Usher1954:56-57]
- rejected the heroic view of invention, but also objected to the determinists’ opposing view
- chose middle ground: building on the work of earlier inventors was fundamental to invention, but in which the individual nevertheless held a crucial place.
- invention was not a heroic feat but a “cumulative accomplishment” that was the result of the “continuous activity of common mental processes” [Usher1954:5-6]

Merton, Robert K.
Singletons and Multiples in Scientific Discovery: A Chapter in the Sociology of Science. Proceedings of the American Philosophical Society 105 (1961): 470-486
- Schmookler and Constant attacked the 1961 paper and the work of the earlier sociological school on the grounds that their “multiple inventions” were not meaningfully equivalent.
- “the sociologists’ disregard for not only historical but also technological specificity had led them to false conclusions about the non-importance of the individual inventor”

Jacob Schmookler, Invention and Economic Growth (Cambridge, MA: Harvard UP, 1966), 189-195; Edward Constant, ‘On the Diversity and Co-Evolution of Technological Multiples: Steam Turbines and Pelton Water Wheels,’ Social Studies of Science 8 (1987): 183-184. Constant later dismissed the determinist argument as ‘simply wrong’. (Origins of the Turbojet Revolution, 2)

- Constant’s notion of “technological revolutions” was enthusiastically accepted by some reviewers as a potential theoretical model of invention, but it has found little use beyond Constant’s own study
- overview of the theory:
- “normal technology” is the technology to which a group of technologists are dedicated
- it’s constantly improved through successive series of small changes within the dominant framework
- if a “normal technology” fails, solutions will be sought first within the existing “normal” system
- a “technological revolution” occurs if a group of technologists becomes committed to a new technology, based on completely different assumptions, and successfully displaces the old “normal technology”

Chapter 2: Turbojet Production During the Second World War

- production during WW2 has not been studied in its own right
- used as a proxy for success in invention and development
- ca. 6600 (production) engines made in Germany through March 45, by the end of 45, the UK made just under 750, the US about 300

63

- the biggest flaw is unproblematic linking of invention and production
- while invention made production possible, it did not make production necessary or inevitable,
- and it did not define exactly what would be produced
- production does not necessaarily indicate technical achievement, except in a very basic way

64

…this chapter will show that German production success rested on the turbojet’s character as an ersatz piston engine, a cheaper more easily produced aero-propulsion system. The turbojet engine was not an existing machine passively and detrimentally affected by the many material shortages that afflicted Germany. Rather it was material shortages, including of metals and aviation fuel, that made poor-quality turbojet engines an attractive weapon.
(Pavelec, Jet Race, pages 32 and 97: engines took low-grade fuel, became easier to make over time, and could use inferior materials)

In 1941-1942, a strong competitor to the W.2B emerged: the H.1, or Halford turbojet engine, designed by the independent aero-engine designer Frank Halford. The H.1 was designed to reach 3,000 pounds of thrust, almost twice as much as the W.2B. This performance would allow a jet fighter with a single engine to outperform the contemporary Typhoon and Spitfire…
In mid-1942, the H.1 seemed to offer greater thrust, reliability and development potential than the W.2B, despite having been designed less than a year earlier.

The remarkable production of ersatz aero-engines

Increasing the ease and reducing the cost of airframe and aero-engine production had been a goal of the RLM since the beginning of the war

103

Jumo and BMW’s turbojet engines could be made using much less material — whether scarce or plentiful metals — than piston engines of a similar power output. The important Daimler-Benz 603 piston engine, for example, produced 1750 horsepower for a dry weight of 2030 pounds; its power to weight ratio was 0.86 hp/lb. The Jumo 004B (the firm’s first production version) in contrast, weighed 1585 pounds (dry) but produced the rough equivalent of 2580 horsepower when flying at 400 mph. Its power to weight ratio at speed was thus about 1.6 hp/lb.

Much is made of the German replacement of strategic metals in their turbojet engines as a virtuoso technical accomplishment (e.g. Constant1980:211), but efforts to reduce the strategic metal content of piston aero-engines were already standard in the first years of the war.

104

- aero-engine production used abt 130 t of Ni/month at the start of 1940
- in 1942, only around 50 t/month
- despite an increase in overall engine production over the same period

- progression to less strategic metals per engine from test to production articles was planned from the start; prototypes used more metals in order to speed up the development
- the crucial point is that the move to turbojets itself saved metal!

- also, manhour savings: Jumo 213, DB 603, and BMW801 all took about 3000 man-hours to build

105

- in UK, careful planning and highly specialised production lines in Manchester allowed Ford to reduce the production time for a Merlin engine from 10000 hours to 2727 hours in three years

By the end of the war, the Jumo 004-B-1 (the main production version) required a mere 700 man-hours to build if production was well organized (down from about 980 in May 1943). The RLM required that BMW bring their turbojet production time to 500 hours. This was not an insanely crazy demand, as it would have been for a piston engine, although the BMW engine required closer to 1000 man-hours to make in practice. The firm reduced its engine construction time largely through the substitution of sheet metal parts for parts that required more time for machining or casting, including its air-cooled turbine blades, which were ultimately folded out of sheet metal.

- also, jet engines could run on less refined and more plentiful diesel oil

107

The rising jet engine production figures achieved at the very end of the war, when production across Germany was faltering, were evidence not of German technical superiority but of the turbojet engine’s ability to be mass-produced under conditions of extreme scarcity using brutal labour practices.

109

- Germany’s turbojet production implied engineering and produciton choices that the Allies neither had to make nor would have made

The German programme ultimately sacrificed future development, performance, quality, and safety for production.
