The design task is to find combinations between elements that “fit” well when put together in a system. Due to dependencies among elements the system’s working cannot solely be understood from the behavior of its parts. For this reason, designers are not able to understand ex ante the working of a technology in all its possible designs (“bounded rationality”). Instead, they have to experiment from some working design and add small changes to this desig. This trial-and-error logic in problem-solving renders product development highly incremental and path-dependent. The complex systems perspective thus sheds new light on earlier evolutionary theories that hold that innovative activity is organised along technological trajectories that are part of technological paradigms (Dosi 1982).

Since competing firms will accept any cost-reducing innovation, changes in input prices are insufficient to explain bias in technological development into a particular direction. Put another way, changes in scarcity may motivate innovation, but a change in scarcity does not guarantee that the technical problems encountered in moving towards a particular input-saving direction, can be resolved (cf. Sahal 1981b:399). Though historical examples exist in which in a sudden scarcity of an input motivated innovations in directions that made technology less dependent on this input, the direction of technical change cannot be explained by changes in input prices alone (Rosenberg 1969).

For more discussion on induced innovation, see Coombs et al (1987).

The adoption of a technology cannot solely be explained by current relative input prices. A technology that is more expensive may still be adopted when the development potential is sufficiently higher compared to technologies that are currently less expensive in terms of input prices. Technology adoption cannot be solely explained by input prices, but requires in addition an analysis of firms’ expectations regarding the future development potential of different technological options. Only in the special case in which all technologies improve at the same rate, input prices alone can be considered sufficient to explain the adoption decisions of firms.

Critique of neoclassical and evolutionary models

Critique 1: The first issue that has been largely ignored in analytical models, both in the neoclassical and in the evolutionary tradition, concerns the conceptualisation of technology as an assembled and complex system of interrelated elements.

Critique 2: the second issue that is largely ignored in modelling is the use of heuristics by designers. In neoclassical theory, the assumption of perfect foresight regarding the pay-off of R&D investment neglects the uncertainty inherent in innovation processes. However, the assumption of randomness in the majority of evolutionary models misses the point that, although inherently uncertain in its outcome, the innovatio process is guided by heuristics focusing R&D to rather specific directions. Thus, although designers can not decide perfectly rationally in what directions to search, they are neither expected to search in a purely random manner. Their search behavior is thus best characterized as bounded rational (Simon 1955, 1969). The important research question then becomes what type of heuristics designers apply to search in an intelligent, non-random way without assuming that they are perfectly informed about the odds of success and failure of different options.

1.3.2 Objective of the study

The model that will be developed in this study can be summarized by the following three features:

  1. Technology is not considered as a “black box” and its functionality is not represented solely by cost efficiency. Instead, the unit of analysis … is technology as an artefact, both in form and function. … is defined technically as an assembled complex system of interrelated elements and functionally as a bundle of quality characteristics from which users select.
  2. Technologically, the range of possible technologies is constrained only by the number of combinations between possible elements. Economically, the range of possible technologies is constrained by user preferences and production costs.
  3. The combinatorial nature of innovation in assembled products poses a problem of complexity. The number of possible combinations increases exponentially with the number of elements in a product. Designers are expected to apply “heuristics” that concentrates search activity in particular parts of a complex technological system at particular stages of development.

2.1 Kauffman’s NK-Model

Complex systems can be defined as systems containing elements that have manu interactions (Simon 1969 [1996]: 184-184). The evolutionary properties of complex systems have been subject of research in theoretical biology (Monod 1971; Kauffman 1989, 1993; Altenberg 1995, 1997; Wagner and Altenberg 1995). In biology, one distinguishes between the genotype and the phenotype of an organism. At the level of the genotype, mutations occur that lead to new variants in a population. At the level of the phenotype, which is the ensemble of traits that make up an organism’s fitness, natural selection operates in terms of differential rates of reproduction. Dependencies among genes imply a complex relation between an organism’s genotype and an organism’s phenotype.
Complexity means here that a mutation in one gene may not only change the functional contribution of the mutated gene to the entire phenotype, but can also affect the functional contributions of interrelated genes to the phenotype. A gene does not simply translate into a particular trait, but operates in conjunction with other genes by regulating other genes’ state of activity.

2.1.2 The description of a system as a string of alleles

Birchenhall (1995), Windrum and Birchenhall (1998) and Cooper (2000) developed related evolutionary models of complex technological systems based on genetic algorithms as developed by Holland (1975, 1992). The important difference between the NK-model and genetic algorithms is that the latter include, apart from mutation, crossover as a variety generating mechanism. This mechanism holds that designs are split in two parts, which are randomly matched with parts of other designs analogous to sexual reproduction in biological evolution. The analogy in technological evolution is that particular solutions in one design can be introduced in another design and vice versa. In the following, cross-over is not explicitly dealt but the possiblity of multi-element mutation, which is conceptually close to crossover, is considered.

2.1.3 Epistatic relations between elements

Dependencies between the functioning of elements in a complex system are called “epistatic relations” (Kauffman 1993:41). An epistatic relation from one element to another element implies that when the allele of the one element changes, this change affects both the functioning of the one element itself and the functioning of the element that it epistatically affects. The ensemble of epistatic relations within a technological system is called a technology’s internal structure (Simon 1969; Saviotti 1986), or, as it will be called in the following, a technology’s architecture (Henderson and Clark 1990).
…

57
When epistatic relations exist between elements, the search for a good design becomes a dififcult task. The introduction of a new allele for one element may increase its functionality, but at the same time it may decrease the functionality of other elemetnts that it epistatically affects. Trade-offs between the functioning of elements in complex systems exist that make it hard to improve the functioning of one element without negative effects on the functioning of other elements (Alexander 1964; Rosenberg 1969). The more epistatic relations exist between elements, the more trade-offs are present in a system, and the more likely innovations in elements have negative by-effects on the workings of other elements.

At the population level of competing firms, selection takes place on two levels. Within the firm, a new variation is developed by means of mutation and this new design variation is accepted when found superior to the previous design or rejected when found inferior to the previous design. This internal variation-and-selection mechanism takes place within each firm until it reaches a local optimum. Then, the firm offers its locally optimal design on the market and competes for users. AT this population level of competing firms, designs are subject to market competition as a second selection mechanism. One can thus think of market competition at the level of the population of firms as a competition in a population of designs that are all locally optimal.

To understand the main evolutionary dunamics at the population level that follow frim the NK-model, one can look at the variance in fitness values of local optima. The variance in fitness values of local optima differs for systems with different degrees of complexity. For systems with high complexity, local optima have similar fitness levels. In the context of biological evolution, a small variance in fitness values of locally optima implies that natural selection
- operates slowly in eliminating less fit
- in technological evolution, small variance implies that market competition between different technological solutions will operate slowly.
- systems wit low complexity have a large variance in the fitness values of local optima
- moreover, fewer local optima exist
-> thus, competition will then operate faster in eliminating suboptimals
- Thus, the speed of evolution is inversely related to the complexity of a system’s architecture (Kauffman 1993:53-54)
A second principle that can be derived from the properties of NK landscapes for population dynamics is that the outcome of hill-climbing on fitness landscapes with high complexity is very sensitive to the initial starting point. The higher the complexity of a system, the stronger path-dependency in search becomes. Therefore, different firms that hill-climb the fitness landscape of a very complex system are likely to find different local optima. By contrast, path-dependency in systems with low complexity is weak. The local optimum that firms find are less sensitive to the initial starting point, since fewer local optima exist and their basin of attraction is larger. Therefore, designs of technologies with low complexity will tend to have many alleles in common. Variety in designs will consequently be smaller.
The two principles combined imply that the higher the complexity of a landscape, the larger the variety in locally optimal designs, and the more persistent this variety due to the low variance in fitness values of local optima.

Apart from saving on the number of required trials, a second advantage of decomposition holds that separate teams can work independently on subsystems and thus in parallel (Marengo 1998; Frenken et al. 1999a; Kollman et al. 2000). This division-of-labor and decentralisation in search activity further economises search time. The K-value, which indicates the number of elements epistatically affecting each element, does not take into account to what extent a system can be decomposed in subsystems. Thus, the K-value is not a precise indicator of complexity in terms of the time needed to find the global optimum. K is a measure of a system’s “structural complexity” as it indicates the complexity in terms of the number of epistatic relations that make of a system’s internal structure. However, the K-value does not indicate the “computational complexity” of a system in terms of the required time to find the global optimum. Systems with the same K-value can have very different degrees of computtational complexity.

2.3.5 Near-decomposability

…what kind of local search strategies, or “heuristics”, are expected to be most successful in finding reasonably good solutions to complex design problems using little computational time and resources.
For one set of complex systems, called nearly-decomposable systems, a very powerful heuristic can be followed. Nearly-decomposable systems are systems that are non-decomposable, but have a structure of epistatic relations such that the majority of these relations are located within subsystems (Simon 1969 [1996]:197-210; Nelson and Winter 1977:53; Dennett 1995[1996]:215)

81

The simulation exercise shows that nearly-decomposable systems are best searched by ignoring the rare epistatic relations between subsystems and by restricting search within subsystems. It thus explains that systems with a nearly-decomposable architecture of epistatic relations are expected to be very common, since for these systems it is relatively easy to find a well-working design. This conclusion has been drawn previously by various scholars in various contexts (Simon 1962, 1969, 1973; Dennett 1995; Schilling 2000). This result confirmed the early discussion of Simon (1969) on nearly-decomposable systems, in which he stated that nearly-decomposable systems have the advantage that these can be improved by changes within each subsystem without the need to coordinate in between subsystems.

The possibility of directed instead of random mutation in human problem-solving constitutes an important difference between technological evolution and biological evolution. In biological evolution, genes have an equal propability to be mutated as mutations are considered “copying errors” in the reproduction proess of genes from parent to offspring. The state of the selection environment bears no influence on the propability of a gene to become subject of mutation. By contrast, human problem-solving in technological systems is generally motivated by improving particular functional attributes of the system. More precisely, before designers engage in search they generally have a mental model of the future user group. The mental model guides search into particular directions rather than in other directions. In modelling terms, the mental model restricts mutations to take place only in particular elements of a system, which are considered instrumental for improvements of particular kinds.

Chapter 5: Entropy statistics as a framework to analyse technological evolution

138
The set of products present in an industry at some point in time is described by the N-dimensional frequency distribution of designs incorporated in the products offered in an industry.
- Saviotto (1996) calls such sets of products "product population"

The main argument made in this chapter holds that entropy statistics applied to frequency distributions of product designs can well describe the evolution of a technology in terms of the changes in the degree and nature of product variety. As an indicator of variety the entropy of the distribution of designs can indicate the extent to which a population is characterised by a "dominant design".
- the analysis of product populations as defined as sets of technological artefacts sharing the same design space differs from market analyses: the latter define product populations in terms of the degree of similarity of the services products provide.
- the empirical analysis in this study deals with the technological evolution of different artefact designs that are all represented in the same design space whatever the differences between different artefacts designs in terms of the level of service characteristics they provide.

- maximum entropy corresponds to the case in which all designs occur at the same frequency
- such a complete flat distribution occurs when designers would randomly move around in design space and randomly choose the alleles of elements
- a skewed distribution occurs when few of the possible designs are dominating the product population
- such a distribution has a low entropy value indicating that product variety is low; designers have not chosen a design at random, but have somehow learned what designs are to be preferred.

The entropy measure thus indicates the degree of variety of product designs in a product population.
- Saviotti (1988b:91) proposed the use of entropy statistics to measure product variety using data of product characteristics. Frenken et al. (1999b, 20000) and Van Herpen and Pieters (2000) actually applied the entropy measure as a measure of product variety using data on product characteristics.
- entropy used in economics e.g. as a measure of industrial concentration as an alternative to the Herfindahl-index (Theil 1967; Zadjenweber 1972; Krehm 1977)
- other applications of entropy statistics include analysis of inequality (theil 1967) and of input-output tables (Theil 1967; Batten 1983)
- in sociology, racial distributions at different levels of aggregation (Theil 1972)
- in innovation studies, as measure of variety in patents and R&D expenditure of different technologies (Grupp 1990; Kodama 1990; Carpenter and Templé 1996)
- in scientometrics, as analyses of word and citation distributions (Leydesdorff 1991, 1992, 1995)

The difference between the total industry entropy and the average firm entropy informs one about the degree of specialisation among firms within an industry.
- there is maximum specialisation when each firm offers only one variety of product designs
- there is minimum specialisation when each firm offers the same variety of product designs
