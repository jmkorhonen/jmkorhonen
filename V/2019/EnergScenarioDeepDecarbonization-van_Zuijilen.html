van Zuijlen, B., Zappa, W., Turkenburg, W., van der Schrier, G., & van den Broek, M. (2019). Cost-optimal reliable power generation in a deep decarbonisation future. Applied Energy, 253, 113587. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.apenergy.2019.113587

.\vanZuijlenCostOptimalDeepDecarbonization.pdf

See also EnergFeasibilityOf100pctREinEU-Zappa

1

There is a growing body of literature studying the transition to a 100% renewable or low or zero carbon power system. In general, these studies show that these power systems are feasible [4–6], under the
condition that there is sufficient transmission [7], backup and storage capacity [8]. The power system will most probably increasingly rely on intermittent renewable energy sources (IRES) [1,9–14], complemented by large-scale low-, zero- or negative-carbon power sources and technologies such as fossil plants without and with CO2 capture and storage (CCS), nuclear power, electricity storage systems and transmission capacity [9], to achieve a reliable supply of electricity. Still, the power system will face several challenges.

2

Going from a net-positive to net-zero or negative emission power system can significantly change the cost-optimal set-up of the power system [14,27]. Thus, long-term planning towards a zero- or net-negative carbon power system may prevent a lock-in of a sub-optimal generation portfolio and stranded assets [14]

The study focusses on the following research question: What are major components of a reliable and cost-optimal electricity system that is both consistent with the Paris Agreement on climate change, and robust enough to deal with variable weather patterns?

5

Demand patterns for 2050 derived from demand patterns of 2015 (ENTSO-E) with an added demand for EVs (579 TWh/a) and heat pumps (262 TWh/a). Heat pump demand profile is based on the outside air temperature derived from the climate model. Total electricity demand is assumed to increase by 33% compared to current levels, to 3400 TWh/a with a system wide peak demand of 636 GW.

Uniform discount rate of 8% is used (on p 6 notes that 8% discount rate gives smaller deviations from real world development than a social discount rate of 3.5%; sensitivity analysis down to 3% performed )

Hydropower fixed at current levels, 162 GW in Western Europe
Geothermal capacity assumed to grow to 50 GW in 2050 in the EU, in line with other high RES studies (10-12).

Battery storage equivalent to 10% of the EV fleet available for load shifting is fixed as an input - total available 125 GW over the whole Western European region; all batteries assumed to have 8 h of storage available, giving a total of 1 TWh battery storage. No additional investment assumed.

Electrolysers and H2 turbines built if this leads to lower system costs. In the core scenarios, H2 can only be generated and stored for later use in the electricity sector. (Additional demand for H2 end use in industry is considered in the sensitivity analysis with a higher electricity demand.)

Storage of H2 assumed to be in salt caverns; each cavern assumed 500 000 m^3, 133 GWh of storage based on lower heating value. Withdrawal rates are limited at 3 kg/s to prevent sudden pressure drops; translates to 360 MW LHV. Assumed round trip efficiency 41%.

Transmission capacity in most scenarios is fixed at levels of what is expected by ENTSO-E in 2027.

5.3. Alternative techno-economic parameters
In most of the scenarios, nuclear power generation delivers between 30% and 45% of total demand despite the current trend in Western Europe where countries are generally turning away from nuclear power. The estimates for future investment costs for nuclear power generation in the future range considerably. However, if the total capital requirement is higher than assumed in the core scenarios (7900 instead of 5300 € kW−1), the economic lifetime of the plant only 40 instead of 60 years and the construction period 10 instead of 7 years, there is no longer a role for nuclear power generation. The remaining electricity demand is covered by an increase in gas fired capacity and IRES capacity.

Assuming the lowest cost estimates from the JRC projections [40], see Table 6, the portfolio changes considerably. The share of IRES almost doubles with the lower cost parameters. With less baseload capacity required, nuclear capacity disappears from the portfolio and CCGT capacity falls as well.

With a lower discount rate of 3% [down from 8%, p. 6] nuclear capacity increases by 44% from 128 GW to 184 GW while PV capacity decreases by 61% from 502 GW to only 195 GW. The installed capacity of other technologies remains similar or decrease slightly compared to the capacities in the Reference scenario.
