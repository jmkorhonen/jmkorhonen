Symbolic meanings, I argue, provide a means to link the concept of culture with the business history of technological innovation. In particular, understanding symbolic meanings sheds new light on the enthusiasm that helps propel new technologies, from the railroad to the Internet, to commercial success. Aluminium benefited from precisely this type of enthusiasm, which was grounded in the sumbolic linking of aluminium with modernity. Yet aluminium also had to contend with contrary meanings, most importantly the perception that it was a cheap substitute, an "ersatz" material. These incompatible meanings came into conflict during competition with alternative materials, with different meanings becoming dominant in different contexts.

Why should business historians care about the symbolic meanings of aluminium, a topic that seems better suited to cultural or art historians? Popular perceptions may influence consumer demand for certain materials, but one would not expect industrial applications to depend in any way on such secondary characteristics. Rather, the history of aluminum seems bound to corporate research, resource endowments, materials markets, industrial structure, and government policy, topics that historians of aluminum have already examined in detail. Indeed, these topics must remain central to the industrial history of aluminum. Yet, without attention to the symbolic meanings of aluminum, this history will remain incomplete. (2)

One powerful type of expectation is technological enthusiasm, which is in essence the symbolic link between a particular technology and the cherished values of a culture. In the Western world this enthusiasm most typically expresses itself as a link between technology and the idea of progress, a recurring phenomenon in business history. Such enthusiasm typically follows a boom-and-bust cycle; railroads, electric power, commercial aviation, and nuclear power have all experienced similar waves of enthusiasm, excess capacity, financial crisis, and disappointment - the Internet is merely the most recent example.(18)
